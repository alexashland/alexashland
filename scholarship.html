Whitman, Correspondence, and the Digital Humanities

  • Link

"'All thy wide geographies': Reading Whitman's Epistolary Database" examines Whitman's social network using a variety of digital methods, including corpus analysis, relationship diagrams, geolocation, and topic modeling. Using correspondence data gathered from the Walt Whitman Archive, we analyze the four major periods of the poet's life: Civil War (1860–1866), Reconstruction (1867–1876), Post-Reconstruction (1877–1887), and Old Age (1888–1892). More specifically, we interrogate the ways in which the poet's commercial endeavors were intimately tied to his epistolary activity. Ultimately, we argue that "[a] networked understanding of Whitmanâ€™s world enables us to problematize the notion of stable public or private arenas, suggesting instead an intertextual and intermedial sphere in which multiple media . . . subjectivities, and cultural formations interact."

Whitman and 19th Century Media

  • Link

"Toward a More Perfect Union: Whitman, Ekphrasis, and the Daguerreotype" explores Whitman's 1855 Leaves of Grass and the ways in which his poetics were shaped by an emerging photorealist aesthetic. I argue that Whitman's aesthetic democracy is articulated through a complicated dialectic of cultural inheritence (painting) and radical democratic individuality (photography). Included in this chapter is an extended close reading of Alfred Jacob Miller's important midcentury painting "The Trapper's Bride," and an analysis of section 10 of Leaves of Grass wherein I see Whitman's rehearsal of classical ekphrastic modes of expression being complemented and challenged by daguerrian techniques and journalistic precision.

Zora Neale Hurston's Racial Geography

  • Link

The concept of "mobility" is typically discussed in terms of geographic traversal and economic opportunity. "Off the Grid: Zora Neale Hurston's Racial Geography in Their Eyes Were Watching God" interrogates this notion of mobility in order to examine the ways in which Hurston complicates the idea of land and property as discoverable—and indeed purchasable—spaces of personal and collective agency. In doing so, I augument existing conversations about mobility to include sexual and narrative territories as well.
