Dissertation

Abstract

The Documentary Turn in Nineteenth-Century U.S. Literature

Overview

This dissertation examines the emergence of documentary aesthetics in nineteenth-century U.S. literature. I argue that the intersection of literary and non-literary texts radically altered, extended, and redefined the limits of what could be expressed in novels, poetry, and phototextual materials. Documents especially were creatively redeployed as a means of more precisely interrogating conceptions of race, gender, class, and nation. Responding to a political climate marked by intense “compromise,” writers of documentary literature deployed “source” material as a means of explicitly engaging the social and political concerns that pressed most urgently on their readers. Primary authors discussed include Harriet Beecher Stowe, William Wells Brown, Herman Melville, and the phototextual albums of Alexander Gardner.

Future Chapters

“'figures cannot lie': Documenting Novel Sources in Antebellum U.S. Literature

An expanded discussion of the ways in which Stowe's documentary asseemblage, curation, and publication influenced her transnational activism and abolitionist work.

"Of 'monuments' and 'secret facts': Documents in Herman Melville’s Short Fiction

An exploration of documentary mediation in Melville's short stories and novellas, including: "Bartleby," "Benito Cereno," and "Billy Budd."

"'this autograph cannot be counterfeited': Racial Legibility and Bioforensics in Mark Twain’s Pudd’nhead Wilson"

This chapter will explore metaphors of inscription and the ways in which racial and gender identities are "written" according to a false binary of biological and social codes.
