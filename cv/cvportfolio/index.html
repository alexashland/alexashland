Dissertation

The Documentary Turn in Nineteenth-Century U.S. Literature

Introduction

"Truth uncompromisingly told": Ruptural Aesthetics in Antebellum U.S. Literature

This dissertation examines the emergence of documentary aesthetics in nineteenth-century U.S. literature. I argue that the intersection of literary and non-literary texts radically altered, extended, and redefined the limits of what could be expressed in novels, poetry, and phototextual materials. Documents especially were creatively redeployed as a means of more precisely interrogating conceptions of race, gender, class, and nation. Responding to a political climate marked by intense “compromise,” writers of documentary literature deployed “source” material as a means of explicitly engaging the social and political concerns that pressed most urgently on their readers.
