Bio

PhD candidate at the University of Iowa. Studying 19th and 20th century American literature. Research Assistant in the Walt Whitman Archive. Teaching Assistant in the Writing Center.

  • Web CV

Alexander Ashland is a PhD candidate in English at the University of Iowa. He specializes in nineteenth-century American literature, and his teaching and research interests include media and visual studies, cultural memory, representations of violence and trauma, poetry and poetics, and Digital Humanities. He has published and presented on Melville, Whitman, and the emergence of documentary aesthetics in mid ninteenth-century U.S. literature. He explores this development more fully in his dissertation, The Documentary Turn in Nineteenth-Century U.S. Literature, 1850–1877.

Additionally, he is the project creator of Mapping Whitman's Correspondence (i.e. "Whitmap"), which he continues to develop along with other members of the Walt Whitman Archive and the University of Iowa's Public Digial Scholarship and Publishing Studio. His forthcoming chapter, "'All thy wide geographies': Reading Whitman's Epistolary Database" (New Whitman Studies, 2019), uses data gathered from this project to analyze the ways in which Whitman's epistolary activity and the geographic distribution of letters influenced his poetry and prose. Whitmap also has been developed from the ground up to be used as a pedagogical tool, offering teachers and students a means of exploring a dataset which has received far less attention than the poet's published materials.

Dissertation

My dissertation examines the emergence of documentary aesthetics in nineteenth-century U.S. literature. I argue that the intersection of literary and non-literary texts radically altered, extended, and redefined the limits of what could be expressed in novels, poetry, and phototextual materials. Documents especially were creatively redeployed as a means of more precisely interrogating conceptions of race, gender, class, and nation. Responding to a political climate marked by intense “compromise,” writers of documentary literature deployed “source” material as a means of explicitly engaging the social and political concerns that pressed most urgently on their readers.
