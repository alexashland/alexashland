Publications

My most recent publication appears in the volume Ekphrasis in American Poetry: The Colonial Period to the 21st Century. My chapter, "Toward a More Perfect Union: Whitman, Ekphrasis, and the Daguerreotype," explores the ways in which the nineteenth-century writer's poetics emerged from his interest in classical painting and new representational media such as the dagaurreotype.

Ultimately, I argue that Whitman's aesthetic democracy is articulated through this complicated dialectic of cultural inheritence and radical democratic individuality. Included in this chapter is an extended close reading of Alfred Jacob Miller's important midcentury painting "The Trapper's Bride".

I couple this reading with an analysis of section 10 of Leaves of Grass wherein I see Whitman's rehearsal of classical ekphrastic modes of expression being complemented and challenged by daguerrian precision.

Dissertation

Much of my research focuses on theories of archive and gallery display, mediation, and how technologies and cultures interact. I’m particularly concerned with how media technology informs and transforms literary imaginations, and and how these landscapes are then subsequently redployed. More specifically, I look at the ways in which material culture responds to, challenges, reinforces, preserves, and rearticulates nationhood and memory during periods of intense transformation and flux.

I position the emergence of modern documentary poetics within a semi-fluid context of nineteenth- and twentieth-century literature. What interests me most is how this poetic mode seems to transcend time and place. Common to each, though, is their proximity to social, political, and economic crises, where the image and thus the literary landscape itself is "captured" by moments of trauma and violence.

Lastly, I look at the possibility of non-documentary literatures to mimic, perform, or emulate documentary poetics.
